Covid cluster

Dek comes here if there will have dek.

By Author2 October 2020

In late February, drug company Biogen held its annual conference in Boston. The United States had fewer than 20 known COVID-19 cases at the time. But one of the roughly 200 attendees must have carried the virus.

Dozens of people fell ill. At least 97 people who attended the conference or lived in a household with someone who did tested positive.

The Biogen meeting had become a ‘superspreading event’. Eventually, the virus spread from the meeting across the state of Massachusetts and to other states. A recent study estimates it led to tens of thousands of cases in the Boston area alone.

COVID-19 superspreading events have been reported around the world. They happen everywhere: At bars and barbecues, gyms and factories, schools, ships, and churches. But why do these disease clusters occur—and why are they so important?

COVID-19 and many other diseases transmit from person to person, which happens more easily if people are in close contact. The reproduction rate, R, determines how fast a disease can spread.

R denotes the number of people infected, on average, by a single infected person. In theory, if R is 2, the number of cases double in every generation: From 1

to 2

to 4, to 8 infected people, and so on.

Real life isn’t that neat.

One person may infect four people.

Two of those may not transmit the disease to anyone else. One might transmit it to 3 others, and the fourth one may infect 21 people.

Of those 21, many may not pass the virus on to anyone else. But perhaps one will pass it on to 17 others.

And so on.

In many infectious diseases, most people infect no-one else. A small percentage of people cause most of the transmission.

How strong this pattern is depends on the disease. A study published in April estimated that in the case of COVID-19, 10% percent of patients are responsible for 80% of the spread.

A study from India published in Science this week (CK) concluded that 8% of infected people caused 60% of the secondary cases. 70% of people did not pass the virus on to anyone else.

A March wedding in Jordan was another tragic example of a superspreading event. The father of the bride was infected; he passed the virus on to at least 76 of around 360 guests, a study says. One woman died.

If a small minority of cases leads to most of the spread, then stopping these people from transmitting should bring the epidemic under control. But how?

One key strategy is to curb superspreading events as much as possible. Researchers have noticed several factors that make superspreading events more likely, known in Japan as the three C’s:

Closed spaces with poor ventilation

Crowds

and Close contact settings.Shouting, singing, and heavy breathing also seem to increase the risk of superspreading.

Paying attention to superspreading events is also important for contact tracing.

If you find someone has COVID-19, you can trace their contacts and test or quarantine them. But there is a high likelihood that they didn’t infect a single other person.

However, there is a high likelihood that that person became infected at a superspreading event.So contact tracers should spend more time understanding where someone was infected and finding other people from the same cluster, health experts say.
This is called backward contact tracing. It can help find more chains of transmission, But it also helps scientists better understand where superspreading is still happening.
